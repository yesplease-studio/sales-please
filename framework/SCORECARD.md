# Deal Quality Checklist

12 binary items across the five WORTH dimensions. Each item is true or not. No partial credit, no "kind of." The point is to force honesty about what you actually know versus what you're hoping is true.

---

## How to score

Go through each item after a discovery call, meeting, or whenever you have new information about a deal. Check the ones that are true. Leave the rest unchecked.

Don't guess. If you don't know whether the economic buyer is identified, the answer is no. The checklist rewards what you've confirmed, not what you assume.

---

## The checklist

### W — Worth pursuing (must-have)

- [ ] **Audience match.** Company fits your defined ICP (segment, size, stage, geography).
- [ ] **Revenue proportionate.** The deal size justifies the time and effort to close it.
- [ ] **Competitive landscape favorable.** You're not walking into a rigged evaluation or a market where someone else has an unfair advantage.

### O — Owner of the decision (accelerator)

- [ ] **Economic buyer identified.** You know, by name, who can sign off on this purchase.
- [ ] **Access exists.** You have direct access to the economic buyer, or a clear path through a champion who will make the introduction.
- [ ] **Champion emerged.** Someone inside the company is actively advocating for your solution.

### R — Real problem, quantified (must-have)

- [ ] **Problem articulated.** The prospect has described the problem in their own words, with specifics.
- [ ] **Cost of status quo known.** They can quantify what the current situation costs them (money, time, risk, or missed opportunity).

### T — Trigger and timeline (accelerator)

- [ ] **Compelling event exists.** There's a specific event or deadline creating urgency (board meeting, funding round, competitive threat, regulatory deadline).
- [ ] **Decision timeframe confirmed.** They've stated when they need to make a decision, and it's concrete.

### H — How we fit (accelerator)

- [ ] **Solution-problem match.** Your offering directly addresses the specific pain they described.
- [ ] **Differentiation understood.** They can articulate why you're different from alternatives they've considered.

---

## Scoring thresholds

Count the checked items and evaluate must-have strength:

| Score | Must-haves | Quality | Action |
|-------|-----------|---------|--------|
| 10-12 | Both W and R strong | **High** | Pursue aggressively. This deal deserves your best effort and fastest follow-up. |
| 6-9 | W and R present | **Medium** | Nurture and work the gaps. Identify which unchecked items you can influence and focus your next conversation there. |
| 0-5 | — | **Low** | Disqualify or deprioritize. Your time is better spent elsewhere. |
| Any | W or R absent | **Not qualified** | Stop. If the fit isn't there or the problem isn't real, no amount of work on other dimensions will save this deal. |

---

## Working the gaps

The checklist isn't just a score. It's a map of what to do next.

Each unchecked item tells you something specific:

- **Unchecked W items:** You need more information about the company, or you need to be honest that this isn't your market.
- **Unchecked O items:** You need to navigate the org. Ask your contact: "Who else needs to be involved in this decision?" and "Can we get them in the next conversation?"
- **Unchecked R items:** You need a deeper discovery conversation. Ask: "What is this costing you today?" and "Can you walk me through a specific example?"
- **Unchecked T items:** You need to find or create urgency. Ask: "What happens if you don't solve this in the next 6 months?" and "Is there a specific deadline driving this?"
- **Unchecked H items:** You need to sharpen your positioning for this account. Ask: "What else have you looked at?" and "What would make this a clear yes for you?"

If you can't move an unchecked item after two attempts, that's information. The gap might be structural, not conversational.
