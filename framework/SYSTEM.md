# WORTH Framework

A lightweight deal qualification framework for founders and small teams selling B2B. Built for people who don't have a sales background but need a system for deciding where to spend their time.

WORTH is not MEDDPICC. It doesn't assume you have a sales org, a CRM team, or a pipeline review cadence. It assumes you're a founder or small team with more opportunities than hours, and you need a fast, honest way to figure out which ones deserve your attention.

---

## Philosophy

Most qualification frameworks were designed for sales teams at scale. They optimize for pipeline reporting, forecast accuracy, and handoff consistency between reps. That's useful if you have reps.

If you're a founder selling your own product, you need something different. You need a framework that helps you think clearly about whether a deal is real, not one that helps a VP of Sales predict quarterly revenue.

WORTH optimizes for three things:

1. **Speed.** You should be able to score a deal in minutes, not hours.
2. **Honesty.** The framework should surface uncomfortable truths early, before you've invested time you can't get back.
3. **Action.** Every score should point to something specific you can do next, or tell you to stop.

## The five dimensions

### W — Worth pursuing

Is this the right kind of company for what you sell?

This is the fit question. Before anything else, does this prospect match your defined audience? Is the revenue potential proportionate to the time you'll invest? Is the competitive landscape favorable, or are you walking into a bake-off you'll lose?

Worth pursuing is a must-have by default. If the fit isn't there, nothing else matters.

**What to look for:**
- Company matches your ICP (segment, size, stage, geography)
- The deal size justifies the sales effort
- You're not competing against someone with an unfair advantage in this account

**Red flags:**
- "We're talking to everyone in this space" (you're column fodder)
- Company is outside your sweet spot but the contact is enthusiastic (enthusiasm isn't fit)
- The deal would require significant customization to close

### O — Owner of the decision

Do you know who makes the buying decision, and can you reach them?

Most stalled deals die here. You're talking to someone who loves what you do but can't sign a contract. The economic buyer is two levels up and doesn't know you exist. Or there's a "committee" that needs to align, which is code for "no one owns this."

**What to look for:**
- Economic buyer identified by name and title
- You have direct access, or a clear path through a champion
- A champion has emerged who will advocate internally

**Red flags:**
- "I need to run this by my team" with no specifics about who or when
- Multiple stakeholders mentioned but no clear decision-maker
- Your contact is enthusiastic but junior

### R — Real problem, quantified

Can they describe the problem in their own words, and do they know what it's costing them?

This is the must-have reality check. If the prospect can't articulate the problem specifically, or if the problem is theoretical rather than felt, the deal isn't real yet. And if they can't quantify the cost of the status quo (in money, time, risk, or missed opportunity), there's no urgency to change.

**What to look for:**
- They describe the problem in concrete, specific terms
- They can articulate what the current situation is costing them
- The problem is active, not hypothetical ("we should probably fix this someday")

**Red flags:**
- Vague problem statements ("we want to improve our process")
- No cost attached to the status quo
- The problem was identified by someone other than the person you're talking to

### T — Trigger and timeline

Is there a compelling event that creates urgency, and do they have a concrete decision timeframe?

Deals without triggers drift. A prospect can love your product, understand the value, and still not buy because nothing is forcing a decision. Triggers create urgency: a board meeting, a funding round, a competitive threat, a regulatory deadline, a key hire starting.

**What to look for:**
- A specific event creating time pressure
- A stated decision timeframe ("we need this in place by Q3")
- Internal momentum (budget allocated, project team formed)

**Red flags:**
- "No rush, we're just exploring"
- Timeline keeps sliding
- The trigger is aspirational rather than real ("we'd like to have this before...")

### H — How we fit

Does your solution actually address their specific problem, and do they understand why you're different from the alternatives?

This is the shape-match question. Even if everything else checks out, if your product doesn't fit their specific pain configuration, you'll either lose the deal or win a customer who churns. And if they don't understand your differentiation, you're competing on price.

**What to look for:**
- Your solution directly addresses the problem they described
- They can articulate why you're different from alternatives they've considered
- The implementation path is realistic (no "we'll figure it out later")

**Red flags:**
- They want you to be something you're not
- They're comparing you feature-by-feature against a competitor with different strengths
- "We just need something that works" (no differentiation = price competition)

## Dimension tiering

Not all dimensions carry equal weight. WORTH uses two tiers:

**Must-have:** If these dimensions are weak, the deal is not qualified regardless of overall score. Default must-haves are **W** (Worth pursuing) and **R** (Real problem, quantified).

**Accelerator:** These dimensions strengthen a deal and help prioritize between qualified opportunities. Default accelerators are **O** (Owner), **T** (Trigger and timeline), and **H** (How we fit).

Tiering is configurable during setup. The defaults work for most B2B founder-selling contexts, but your situation might be different. If you sell into enterprise with long procurement cycles, you might make T (Trigger and timeline) a must-have. If you're in a crowded market, H (How we fit) might be a must-have.

## Using WORTH

WORTH is designed to be used at three speeds:

1. **Gut Check** (30 seconds) — Five yes/no questions before you book a call. See `framework/GUT-CHECK.md`.
2. **Deal Qualification** (5-10 minutes) — Score a deal against the full checklist after a discovery call. See `framework/SCORECARD.md`.
3. **Deal Review** (ongoing) — Update scores as the deal progresses. Catch deals that are stalling or improving.

The framework lives in your deal files (`deals/`). Each deal gets scored against the checklist, and the scores tell you where to focus your next conversation.

---

## What WORTH is not

- **Not a CRM.** Your deal files are your CRM. WORTH is the thinking framework, not the database.
- **Not a forecast tool.** It doesn't predict close rates or revenue. It helps you decide where to spend time.
- **Not a script.** It doesn't tell you what to say on a call. It tells you what to listen for.
- **Not permanent.** Your tiering, your checklist items, and your thresholds should evolve as you learn what actually predicts closed deals in your context.
